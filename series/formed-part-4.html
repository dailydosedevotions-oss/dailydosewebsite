Part 4 — Discipline: Choosing What Leads to Life

Releases Sunday Jun 28, 2026 at 7:00PM

1 Corinthians 9:27 (ESV)

“But I discipline my body and keep it under control, lest after preaching to others I myself should be disqualified.”

Discipline is not a word most people naturally enjoy. It often sounds restrictive, uncomfortable, or exhausting. Yet throughout Scripture, discipline is not presented as punishment but as training. It is the intentional choosing of what leads to life, even when something else feels easier in the moment.

The truth is, everyone is being shaped by something.

Habits shape people. Thought patterns shape people. What is consumed daily shapes people. What people continually return to in secret eventually forms the direction of their lives.

That is why discipline matters spiritually. Without it, emotions often begin leading decisions instead of truth. People begin drifting wherever comfort, distraction, impulse, or feeling carries them.

But discipline helps create consistency where feelings constantly fluctuate.

Paul understood this deeply when he wrote:

1 Corinthians 9:24-25 (ESV)

“Do you not know that in a race all the runners run, but only one receives the prize? So run that you may obtain it. Every athlete exercises self-control in all things. They do it to receive a perishable wreath, but we an imperishable.”

Paul compares spiritual discipline to training. An athlete does not become strong accidentally. There is repetition, sacrifice, structure, consistency, and perseverance involved. In the same way, spiritual growth rarely happens through occasional moments alone. It is often formed quietly through daily decisions repeated over time.

I know in my own life there were years where my decisions were completely led by impulse, escape, and whatever brought temporary relief quickest. Drugs, alcohol, and destructive patterns slowly trained my mind and heart to constantly look for comfort instead of truth. Discipline felt almost impossible because everything in me wanted immediate relief over lasting change.

And honestly, that mindset does not always disappear overnight.

Even after coming to Christ, there were still moments where inconsistency, distraction, and old ways of thinking tried to pull me backwards. Moments where comfort felt easier than surrender. Moments where feeding the flesh felt easier than denying it.

That is why discipline matters so deeply.

Not because discipline saves anyone, but because without discipline people often drift back toward the very things God is trying to free them from.

Many people want the fruit of a disciplined life with God, but resist the quiet daily surrender that produces it. We often want transformation while still protecting the comforts that keep us unchanged.

This is where many people struggle. Discipline usually becomes hardest when motivation disappears. It is easy to pray when passion feels strong. It is easy to read Scripture when emotions feel high. But there are also ordinary days. Dry days. Tired days. Distracted days.

And often those are the days where discipline matters most.

Because discipline keeps someone moving toward God even when feelings are unstable.

This does not mean becoming robotic or legalistic. Discipline without relationship quickly becomes empty religion. The goal is not perfection or appearance. The goal is remaining rooted in Christ instead of constantly being controlled by changing emotions and desires.

Jesus Himself lived with intentionality. Throughout the Gospels, we repeatedly see Him withdrawing to pray, remaining obedient to the Father, and refusing to be controlled by pressure, temptation, or public opinion.

Luke 9:23 (ESV)

“And he said to all, ‘If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me.’”

Notice the word daily.

Following Jesus is not only built in dramatic moments. Much of spiritual formation happens in ordinary daily choices:

choosing prayer when distracted

choosing truth when tempted

choosing forgiveness when hurt

choosing humility when pride rises

choosing obedience when compromise feels easier

Over time, discipline helps strengthen what the flesh naturally resists.

There will be moments where discipline feels difficult. Moments where consistency feels slow. Moments where growth feels unseen. But seeds planted faithfully over time eventually produce fruit.

Discipline is not about earning God’s love.

It is about learning to walk steadily with Him.

And often the quiet disciplines nobody sees become the very places where God shapes a person most deeply.
