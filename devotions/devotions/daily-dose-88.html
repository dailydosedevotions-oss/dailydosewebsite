Daily Dose #88

July 21, 2026

GOD'S RESTORATION

JOEL 2:25–27 (ESV)

“I will restore to you the years that the swarming locust has eaten, the hopper, the destroyer, and the cutter, my great army, which I sent among you. You shall eat in plenty and be satisfied, and praise the name of the LORD your God, who has dealt wondrously with you. And my people shall never again be put to shame.”

One of the hardest things to live with is the feeling that time has been wasted. Many people look back over parts of their lives and see missed opportunities, poor decisions, broken relationships, years lost to destructive habits, or seasons where they drifted far from God. Regret has a way of convincing us that what has been lost can never be recovered.

Yet Joel gives God's people a remarkable promise. The Lord says, “I will restore to you the years that the swarming locust has eaten.” The locusts had caused devastation throughout the land. What once produced life and fruit had been stripped bare. The people were left looking at the damage and wondering what could possibly come next.

God's response was not simply to repair what had been damaged. His response was restoration.

Biblical restoration is more than returning something to its previous condition. Throughout Scripture, God often restores in ways that reveal His grace, His power, and His faithfulness. He takes what has been broken and uses it for His purposes. He brings hope where there was despair and life where there once seemed to be loss.

This does not mean every consequence of our past immediately disappears. It does not mean every difficult situation is instantly reversed. What it does mean is that God is able to redeem what seems beyond redemption. The years that feel wasted are not beyond His reach.

We see this truth throughout the Bible. Joseph was betrayed by his brothers and sold into slavery, yet God used those events to position him for a greater purpose. Peter denied Jesus three times, yet Christ restored him and used him powerfully in the early church. The common thread is not human faithfulness but God's faithfulness.

This is why ROMANS 8:28 (ESV) says, “And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose.” God does not waste anything that is surrendered to Him. Even painful chapters can become part of a testimony that points others toward His grace.

Joel's promise also points beyond material restoration. The passage repeatedly draws attention back to God Himself. The people would be satisfied, praise the name of the Lord, and know that He was among them. Restoration was never simply about receiving blessings. It was about being restored to a right relationship with God.

This is an important reminder because sometimes we focus more on what we want God to restore than on the God who restores. His greatest desire is not merely to improve our circumstances. His greatest desire is to draw us closer to Himself.

There may be areas of your life today where you feel the effects of loss. Perhaps there are opportunities you wish you had taken, decisions you wish you had not made, or years that seem to have disappeared. Joel reminds us that God is still able to restore. He is still able to redeem. He is still able to bring beauty from ashes and hope from broken places.

The story is not over simply because a difficult chapter exists.

The God who restores is still writing the story.

And His grace is greater than anything that has been lost.
