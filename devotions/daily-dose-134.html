Daily Dose #134

September 5, 2026

MERCY IN THE MIDDLE

This devotion is scheduled and will become available at 7am on September 5, 2026.

PSALM 13:5 (ESV)

"But I have trusted in your steadfast love; my heart shall rejoice in your salvation."

Psalm 13 begins with questions that sound painfully honest. David asks, "How long?" He does not hide his sorrow, confusion, or heaviness from God. Yet in the middle of lament, he turns toward mercy.

Faith does not mean you never ache. Faith means you bring the ache to God. Lament is not rebellion when it is poured out before the Lord with trust. It is the soul refusing to suffer alone.

There may be seasons when joy feels far away and answers feel delayed. In those moments, we need more than shallow encouragement. We need the mercy of God in the middle of the pain.

David says, "I have trusted in your steadfast love." Not in his circumstances. Not in his ability to understand. Not in how quickly things changed. He anchors himself in God's mercy.

If you are in the middle today, pray honestly. Tell the Lord where it hurts. Ask the hard questions in His presence. Then let mercy have the last word.

Psalm 13 teaches us that lament can be faithful. David asks hard questions, but he asks them toward God, not away from Him. That direction matters. Pain can either drive us into isolation or drive us into honest prayer.

Romans 8:38-39 says that neither death nor life, nor anything else in all creation, "will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord." That truth does not make every circumstance easy, but it gives the suffering believer something solid beneath their feet.

When David says he has trusted in God's steadfast love, he is not pretending the sorrow is gone. He is choosing where the final word will rest. Not on the delay. Not on the heaviness. Not on the unanswered question. On mercy.

Jesus Himself cried out from the cross with the words of Psalm 22:1: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Scripture gives language for anguish because God is not offended by honest suffering brought before Him.

But lament in the Bible often turns. It may turn slowly, through tears, but it turns toward remembrance: God has been faithful. God is merciful. God saves. That turn may not change the circumstance at once, but it anchors the soul while the storm is still present.

Psalm 147:3 says, "He heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds." Nearness is not always felt as an immediate change in circumstance. Sometimes it is the quiet strength to breathe, pray, and endure another day under mercy.

Your lament can become worship when it turns toward the God who saves.
